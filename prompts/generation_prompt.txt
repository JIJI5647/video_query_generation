PROMPT VERSION: generation_prompt_caption_v6

TASK:
You are given ALL of the captions extracted from one video, plus the spoken-dialogue
transcript of that video. Each caption describes an observable moment grounded to a
``time_range`` ([start, end] in seconds) of the video. These captions are UNFILTERED:
many will have emotion "neutral" or "unrelevant", or a low ``confidence`` / "weak" or
"ambiguous" ``evidence_strength`` — those carry no usable emotional evidence and you
MUST ignore them. First read every caption (and the transcript) to understand the whole
video, then write high-precision emotion-related temporal grounding queries for human
annotation, each grounded in captions that show a real emotion (one of the eight emotion
labels) with clear, observable evidence. Aim to produce 8 queries. Prefer grounding each
query in a DIFFERENT strong caption so the queries cover the video broadly. If there are
not enough strong captions to reach 8 distinct-caption queries, reuse a strong caption to
write additional queries that approach the same moment from a DIFFERENT angle or
query_type (e.g. one explicit_event, one emotion_state, and one evidence_cue about the
same moment).

You do NOT see the video. Treat the captions and transcript as the ground truth of what
is observable.

VIDEO ID: {video_id}

QUERY TYPE DEFINITIONS:
- explicit_event: an observable emotional action, interaction, or audio event.
  Example: "When does the man in the white shirt raise his voice while speaking to the woman in the red dress?"
- emotion_state: a person's perceived emotional state. You may hedge with "appears",
  "looks", "seems", or "sounds", but hedging is optional, not required.
  Example: "At what point does the woman in the red dress appear upset while listening to the man?"
- evidence_cue: an observable cue that supports emotion reasoning (facial expression,
  voice, body movement, gaze, posture, hesitation, silence, interactional response).
  Example: "Which moment shows the woman avoiding eye contact after the man speaks?"

CAPTIONS (each is grounded to a ``time_range`` in seconds; ``confidence`` and
``evidence_strength`` indicate how reliable the caption is):
{captions_json}

SPOKEN-DIALOGUE TRANSCRIPT (each line is the transcribed speech for a ``time_range`` in
seconds; use it as additional context, aligned to the caption time ranges, to understand
what is said and the emotional tone — but ground queries in OBSERVABLE evidence, not in
plot inferred only from words). Non-verbal sounds (shouting, crying, laughing, gasping,
trembling voice, silence) are described in the captions' ``sound`` field, not here:
{transcript_json}

GENERATION RULES:
1. Every query must be high-precision: only write a query when the captions give clear,
   observable evidence that an annotator could locate. Do NOT lower this bar to hit the
   count — never invent evidence to pad the list. Only ground queries in captions whose
   emotion is one of the eight emotion labels AND whose evidence_strength is "clear"
   (avoid confidence "low" and evidence_strength "weak"/"ambiguous").
2. Generate exactly 8 queries whenever the captions support it. Produce fewer ONLY when
   even after reusing strong captions across different angles/types you cannot reach 8
   without inventing evidence. First exhaust DISTINCT captions, then reuse strong
   captions from different angles to fill the remaining slots up to 8.
3. Every query MUST be grounded in one or more of the captions above. Set the query's
   ``time_range`` to the [start, end] span that covers exactly those captions' time
   ranges (in seconds). A query may span several captions' ranges, and they need NOT be
   adjacent — choose the smallest range that covers the evidence you rely on. Use only
   time ranges that fall within the captions above; do not invent a range with no caption.
4. Do not invent events, people, or cues that are not present in the captions.
5. CAPTION EMOTION IS A CANDIDATE INTERPRETATION, NOT A MUST-COPY ANSWER. The caption's
   ``emotion`` label is one possible reading of the observable evidence — it is not a
   gold label you must reproduce. When the ``emotion`` label disagrees with the caption's
   own observable evidence (``person`` / ``action`` / ``sound`` / ``observable_evidence``),
   or when that evidence is ambiguous or supports more than one emotion, you MAY:
   (a) choose a more fitting emotion word, (b) use a more neutral phrasing, or (c) write a
   pure behavior/cue query (explicit_event or evidence_cue) that names only the observable
   action or cue. Do NOT blindly copy the caption's emotion label. You still must NOT
   introduce any event, person, or cue that is absent from the captions — grounding stays
   tied to observable evidence.
6. Spread queries across explicit_event, emotion_state, and evidence_cue. When you reuse
   the same caption for more than one query, give those queries DIFFERENT query_types so
   each looks at the moment from a distinct angle (the event, the perceived state, the
   observable cue).
7. Use natural, varied question starters — do not start every query the same way:
   "When does...", "At what point...", "Which part of the video...",
   "Where in the video...", "What moment shows...", "How does the video show...".
8. Use the caption's concrete person description (clothing, role, position) — avoid vague
   references like "someone" or "the person" when a better description exists.

QUERY TEXT POLICY (apply to every query_text):
9. Language: write every query_text entirely in English.
10. Anonymization: no character names, actor names, celebrity names, place names, or brand
    names. Replace names with visual descriptions. Role words (doctor, judge) and visually
    grounded kinship terms (mother, husband) are allowed.
11. No timestamps: query_text must NOT contain explicit times or ranges
    (e.g. "at 1:23", "in the first 10 seconds", "near the end"). The ``time_range`` field
    carries the timing; the query_text itself must read naturally without it.
12. Self-contained: the query_text must be understandable on its own, without the caption
    metadata or the time_range — name the person by visual description and put the key
    observable cue into the sentence.

OUTPUT REQUIREMENTS:
Return valid JSON only. No markdown, no comments, no text outside the JSON object.
